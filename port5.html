Comparative Study on Different Types of Motherboard

Introduction

The motherboard, or mainboard, is the central printed circuit board (PCB) that connects and facilitates communication among a computer's critical components, such as the CPU, memory modules, and chipsets (Tomljanović et al., 2013). It also provides slots for expansion cards and controllers, ensuring the components work together harmoniously (Tri, 2020). The motherboard's form factor defines its physical dimensions, layout, and compatibility with computer cases and hardware. Standard form factors like AT, ATX, and Mini-ITX cater to various needs, influencing the number of CPU sockets, memory slots, PCI expansion slots, and integrated features it can support (Eli & Eli, 2024).

Discussion

The motherboard includes four key components: the chipset, which manages data flow between the CPU, memory, and peripherals; the CPU socket, which ensures compatibility with the processor; expansion slots for adding components like graphics cards; and memory slots for RAM, enabling the CPU to access temporary storage (Sabri et al., 2018). These elements define the system's capabilities and performance. The motherboard's form factor determines its dimensions, layout, and compatibility with cases and components, evolving over time to meet needs for compactness, expandability, or performance (Teja, 2024). The AT form factor, introduced by IBM in the 1980s, was large (12 x 13.8 inches) and lacked integrated peripheral ports, eventually being replaced by more compact designs (Teja, 2024).

AT (Advanced Technology) Motherboard

  • • Introduced by IBM in the 1980s, becoming a standard for early PCs.

  • • Measures 12 x 13.8 inches, making it too large for compact cases.

  • • Had limited peripheral support with a single 5-pin DIN connector for keyboards.

  • • Replaced by more compact and versatile designs (Teja, 2024).

ATX (Advanced Technology eXtended) Motherboard

  • • Developed by Intel in 1995 as a benchmark for modern motherboards.

  • • Measures 12 x 9.6 inches, offering better airflow and component accessibility.

  • • Features multiple CPU sockets, RAM slots, and PCIe slots, supporting advanced functions.

  • • Popular due to its balance of size, expandability, and compatibility.

BTX (Balanced Technology eXtended) Motherboard

  • • Designed by Intel to improve thermal management in PCs.

  • • Optimized layout with CPU positioned near intake fan for better cooling.

  • • Failed to gain widespread adoption and was largely replaced by ATX.

Extended-ATX (E-ATX) Motherboard

  • • A larger variant of ATX, measuring up to 12 x 13 inches.

  • • Commonly used in high-performance systems like servers and gaming rigs.

  • • Offers more PCIe slots and greater RAM capacity, but requires larger cases and is more expensive.

LPX (Low Profile eXtended) Motherboard

  • • Designed for slim desktop cases using riser cards for space-saving.

  • • Limited by poor cooling and restricted upgrade options.

  • • Replaced by more efficient form factors like Micro-ATX and Mini-ITX.

Micro-ATX Motherboard

  • • A smaller version of ATX, measuring 9.6 x 9.6 inches.

  • • Compatible with ATX cases but offers fewer expansion slots (typically 3-4).

  • • Ideal for budget builds or compact desktops, balancing performance and size.

Mini-ITX Motherboard

  • • A very compact form factor, measuring 6.7 x 6.7 inches.

  • • Primarily used in specialized, small systems like home theater PCs or embedded systems.

  • • Supports modern processors but has limited expansion slots and RAM capacity.

Mini-ATX and Pico BTX Motherboards

  • • Mini-ATX is a smaller alternative to ATX for compact cases.

  • • Pico BTX is an ultra-compact version of BTX, designed for small systems.

  • • Both are niche form factors with limited mainstream use.
